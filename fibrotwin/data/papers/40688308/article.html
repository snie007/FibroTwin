Abstract

Background

Atrial fibrillation (AF) is closely associated with atrial fibrosis and is primarily driven by cardiac fibroblast-mediated extracellular matrix (ECM) production. Tissue inhibitor of metalloproteinases-1 (TIMP1), a key ECM regulator, has been implicated in cardiovascular fibrosis, but its role in AF remains unclear. This study examined the function of TIMP1 in the angiotensin II (ang II)-induced activation of rat cardiac fibroblasts.

Methods

Differentially expressed gene (DEG) and functional enrichment analyses were performed using the GSE115574 dataset. Key gene modules were identified via protein-protein interaction (PPI) network analysis, and critical module intersection gene expression was analyzed in AF samples from the GSE115574 and GSE14975 datasets. Different concentrations and times of ang II administration were used to treat rat cardiac fibroblasts to mimic AF pathology, and their effects on TIMP1 expression and ang II-induced proliferation and related fibrosis pathways were investigated via transfection, quantitative polymerase chain reaction, Western blotting (WB), and Cell Counting Kit-8 (CCK-8), and colony formation assays.

Results

Functional enrichment analysis showed that DEGs were enriched in collagen fibrillar organization and ECM-receptor interactions. Seven essential intersecting genes (BGN, COL1A1, COL1A2, COL3A1, FMOD, THBS2, and TIMP1) identified from the PPI network analysis were highly expressed in AF samples from the GSE115574 and GSE14975 datasets. In vitro studies revealed ang II-induced upregulation of TIMP1 expression and activation of the TGF-β/Smad pathway. Knockdown of TIMP1 enhanced ang II-induced proliferation of rat cardiac fibroblasts and upregulated the expression of fibrotic markers and proteins related to the TGF-β/Smad signaling pathway.

Conclusions

Our study demonstrated that TIMP1 regulates myocardial fibrosis through the TGF-β/Smad pathway, particularly affecting cardiac fibroblast proliferation. Our findings provide new insights into the molecular process of AF and may offer new targets for therapeutic strategies.

Keywords: Tissue inhibitor of metalloproteinases-1 (TIMP1), atrial fibrillation (AF), TGF-β/Smad pathway, atrial fibrosis, angiotensin II (ang II)

Highlight box.

Key findings

• Through bioinformatic analysis of public datasets, tissue inhibitor of metalloproteinases-1 (TIMP1) was identified as a hub gene highly expressed in atrial fibrillation (AF).

• Angiotensin II (ang II) upregulated TIMP1 expression in rat cardiac fibroblasts and activated TGF-β/Smad pathway.

• TIMP1 knockdown inhibited ang II-induced fibroblast proliferation and fibrosis marker expression, while overexpression had the opposite effect.

What is known and what is new?

• Atrial fibrosis plays a central role in AF progression, and extracellular matrix remodeling contributes to fibrotic changes.

• This study demonstrates that TIMP1 promotes ang II-induced cardiac fibroblast proliferation and fibrosis through the TGF-β/Smad pathway, revealing its potential as a novel target in AF.

What is the implication, and what should change now?

• TIMP1 is a key regulator of cardiac fibroblast activation and fibrosis in AF and may serve as a promising therapeutic target.

• Targeting TIMP1 or its downstream pathways could help attenuate atrial structural remodeling and improve AF treatment strategies.

Introduction

Atrial fibrillation (AF) is characterized by irregular and often rapid contractions of the atria (1). It is commonly associated with cardiovascular disease, hypertension, and diabetes (2). AF is a common disease worldwide, with its morbidity and mortality rates continuing to rise in recent years (3). This condition can lead to various complications, such as thromboembolic stroke, heart failure, and other cardiovascular issues (4). Currently, the main treatment approaches for AF include pharmacotherapy, electrophysiological procedures, and surgical intervention, and are obviously not without potential adverse effects (5). A study has suggested that fibrosis plays a crucial role in sustaining the re-entrant drivers that maintain arrhythmias (6). Therefore, a better understanding of preventing fibrosis could reveal new targets for innovative treatment strategies.

Angiotensin II (ang II) is a key mediator in the development of atrial fibrosis and AF. It has been shown to promote atrial remodeling by modulating ion channels and currents, leading to electrical and structural changes that increase the susceptibility to AF (7). Specifically, ang II activates the TGF-β1/Smad signaling pathway, which drives fibroblast proliferation and extracellular matrix (ECM) deposition, contributing to atrial fibrosis (8,9). Studies have demonstrated that ang II-induced fibrosis leads to increased atrial stiffness and conduction abnormalities, which are critical factors in the development and maintenance of AF. A recent study by Shi et al. showed that ang II infusion in mice resulted in significant atrial fibrosis and increased AF vulnerability, highlighting the role of ang II in AF pathogenesis (9). Another study demonstrated that the administration of the PGI2 analog iloprost inhibited ang II-induced atrial fibrosis and reduced AF inducibility in a mouse model (10). These findings underscore the importance of ang II in driving atrial fibrosis and AF. Therefore, we simulated AF by treating cells with ang II.

AF is closely related to atrial fibrosis, which as an alteration of atrial tissue structure, may lead to electrical conduction abnormalities through the remodeling of the ECM, contributing to AF onset and persistence (11). Tissue inhibitor of metalloproteinases-1 (TIMP1), a member of the TIMP family of proteins, is essential for controlling the activity of matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs). The ECM, a complex network of proteins providing structural support to cells, is targeted by MMPs, which break it down. TIMP1 is an inhibitor that binds to MMPs, modulating their activity and preventing excessive ECM degradation (12). It impacts numerous physiological processes, including immune response, wound healing, and tissue remodeling. Additionally, TIMP1 has been linked to pathological disorders such as cancer, fibrosis, and cardiovascular illnesses, in which dysregulation of matrix remodeling processes occurs (13,14). Several studies have examined the impact of TIMP1 on patients with AF. Li et al. discovered that patients with AF who had higher serum levels of TIMP1 before radiofrequency ablation therapy were at a significantly increased risk of experiencing a recurrence of AF post-treatment, notably when TIMP1 levels surpassed 124.15 ng/mL (15). Sun et al. also reported that higher serum levels of TIMP1 in patients with AF may predict increased atrial fibrosis and be an independent predictor of AF recurrence after radiofrequency ablation therapy (16). Fragão-Marques et al.’s research indicated an elevated production of collagen III and atrial fibrosis in AF patients with severe aortic stenosis, characterized by ECM remodeling and a decreased MMP16/TIMP4 ratio (17). Additionally, these patients exhibited elevated serum levels of TIMP1 and TIMP2 proteins. Therefore, clarifying the role of TIMP1 in inflammation and fibrosis is crucial for understanding its impact on patients with AF.

This work aimed to delineate the regulatory processes of TIMP1 in the pathophysiology of AF and to assess its potential as a therapeutic target. We used bioinformatics to examine TIMP1 expression in AF samples from the GSE115574 and GSE14975 datasets. In vitro research was conducted to determine the influence of ang II on TIMP1 expression in rat cardiac fibroblasts and to evaluate its subsequent effects on fibrosis signaling pathways. Furthermore, we investigated the impact of TIMP1 expression regulation on fibrosis marker expression and cell proliferation, with the aim of discovering novel intervention strategies for AF. We present this article in accordance with the MDAR reporting checklist (available at https://jtd.amegroups.com/article/view/10.21037/jtd-2025-1088/rc).

Methods

Data sources and collection

The AF-related datasets (GSE115574 and GSE14975) were obtained from the Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) public repository (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/). The GSE115574 dataset consists of 28 sinus rhythm (SR) samples, regarded as the normal group, and 31 permanent AF samples, deemed the AF group. The GSE14975 dataset includes 5 AF samples and 5 SR samples, with the former being the AF group and the latter the normal group.

Screening and functional enrichment analysis of differentially expressed genes (DEGs)

Using the GEO2R tool (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/geo2r/), we analyzed the GSE115574 dataset to identify the DEGs. When conducting AF-related gene expression studies, we referenced the fold change (FC) thresholds used in a similar study (18) to ensure that our screening criteria captured the biologically meaningful gene expression changes. Our goal was to select genes with sufficiently significant changes that could potentially impact the pathological process of AF while avoiding false-positive results due to noise. We also cross-validated the expression of critical genes through the GSE14975 dataset to ensure the reproducibility and reliability of our results. Our study selected DEGs based on their expression differences between AF and standard SR samples. We set the FC threshold for upregulated DEGs to be greater than 1.3 and that for downregulated DEGs to be less than 0.77. In addition, we used a P value of less than 0.05 as the significance criterion to further ensure that the expression changes of the selected genes were statistically significant. The identified DEGs were submitted to the Database for Annotation, Visualization, and Integrated Discovery (DAVID; https://david.ncifcrf.gov/) for functional enrichment analysis. Extensive investigations were conducted using Gene Ontology (GO) and the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathways to reveal related functions and pathways. Only findings with P values less than 0.05 were considered significant.

Protein-protein interaction (PPI) network analysis and critical gene identification

After identifying 224 DEGs, we used Cytoscape software (version 3.8.0) to construct a PPI network of the proteins encoded by these DEGs. The Cytohubba plugin within Cytoscape was then employed to identify three key network modules through algorithms including maximum clique centrality (MCC), degree, and maximum neighborhood component (MNC). We performed intersection analysis using the online tool provided by Bioinformatics & Evolutionary Genomics (http://bioinformatics.psb.ugent.be/webtools/Venn/) to obtain essential intersection genes. Following this, we analyzed the expression of these intersection genes in AF samples from both the GSE115574 and GSE14975 datasets, comparing their expression differences between the AF and SR samples.

Ethical statement

The study was conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki and its subsequent amendments. The data used in this study were obtained from public databases (GSE115574 and GSE14975), which contain anonymized human samples. No additional ethical approval was required for the use of these publicly available datasets.

Cell culture and treatment

Rat primary cardiac fibroblasts were purchased from Stemcell Biotechnology Co., Ltd. (cat. no. STM-CE-3303; Shanghai, China; https://www.stemrecell.com/primary-cell-rat-fibroblast/cardiacmuscle.html). The cells (5×105 cells per well) were cultured in Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium (DMEM; Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA), supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) and 1× penicillin/streptomycin (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Culture conditions were maintained at 37 ℃ in a 5% CO2 atmosphere. Additionally, we simulated AF pathological conditions by inducing rat cardiac fibroblasts with various concentrations of ang II (1 nM, 10 nM, 100 nM, and 1 µM) at different time points (12, 24, and 48 hours). The selection of these concentrations and time points was based on published studies (19,20) and our preliminary experimental validation, which confirmed that these conditions effectively induced significant biological responses in rat cardiac fibroblasts.

Transfection assay

To further elucidate the role of TIMP1 in AF pathology, we performed gene knockdown and overexpression assays. We used three distinct sequences of small interfering RNAs (siRNAs) to reduce the TIMP1 expression levels specifically. The siRNA sequences are as follows: si-TIMP1-1, 5'-CAUCCUCUUUGUUGCUAUCAUUGAUA-3' (sense sequence) and 5'-UAUCAAUGAUAGCAACAAGAGGAUG-3' (antisense sequence); si-TIMP1-2, 5'-GCCGUUUUAAGGAACGGAAAUUUGCA-3' (sense sequence) and 5'-UGCAAAUUUUCCGUUUCCUUUAAACGGC-3' (antisense sequence); and si-TIMP1-3, 5'-GAAUCCAGAUUUGUGCACCUGGCAA-3' (sense sequence) and 5'-UUGCCAGGUGCACAAAUCUGGAUUC-3' (antisense sequence). Meanwhile, TIMP1 overexpression was achieved through construction of an expression vector containing the full-length coding sequence of the TIMP1 gene. We used a final siRNA concentration of 50 nM and a plasmid DNA concentration of 2 µg/mL during transfection. Following the manufacturer’s protocol, transfections were carried out using Lipofectamine 3000 reagent (Invitrogen, Thermo Fisher Scientific). After transfection, cells were incubated for 48 hours before subsequent assays and analyses were conducted, allowing for a sufficient amount of time for the optimal expression levels to be reached.

Quantitative real-time polymerase chain reaction assay

Following the manufacturer’s instructions, we isolated total RNA from rat cardiac fibroblasts using the TRIzol reagent (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Complementary DNA (cDNA) synthesis was performed using the PrimeScript RT kit (Takara Bio, Kusatsu, Japan). Quantitative real-time polymerase chain reaction (qRT-PCR) was performed as described in the article by Shahrajabian et al. (21). GAPDH served as the internal control for standardizing the amplification data. The primer sequences used are listed in Table 1. Expression analysis was conducted using GAPDH as the internal control and the 2-ΔΔCT method.

Western blotting assay

Protein lysates from rat atrial fibroblasts were produced by adding protease and phosphatase inhibitors to RIPA lysis solution (Thermo Fisher Scientific). The protein content was determined using the BCA Protein Assay Kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Following this, sodium dodecyl sulfate–polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis-separated proteins equal portions were applied in equal portions to polyvinylidene fluoride membranes (Thermo Fisher Scientific). The membranes were then incubated with the following primary antibodies: TGF-β1 (1:1,000; cat. no. ab215715; Abcam, Cambridge, UK), TIMP1 (1:2,000; cat. no. 30869-1-AP; Proteintech, Rosemont, IL, USA), α-SMA (1:5,000; cat. no. 14395-1-AP; Proteintech), collagen I (1:5,000; cat. no. 66761-1-Ig; Proteintech), collagen III (1:1,000; cat. no. 22734-1-AP; Proteintech), Smad2/3 (1:1,000; cat. no. ab202445; Abcam), and p-Smad2/3 (1:1,000; cat. no. ab272332; Abcam). GAPDH was probed as a housekeeping protein using an antibody diluted at 1:10,000 (cat. no. ab125247; Abcam). After incubation with the primary antibody and subsequent washing with Tris-buffered saline with Tween20 (TBST), the membranes were incubated with horseradish peroxidase (HRP)-conjugated goat anti-rabbit IgG (1:1,000; cat. no. ab21058; Abcam) or goat anti-mouse IgG (1:5,000; cat. no. ab20272; Abcam) for 1 hour at room temperature. Chemiluminescent detection was performed using an enhanced chemiluminescence kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific), and signals were visualized and recorded with a chemiluminescence imaging system (Bio-Rad Laboratories, Hercules, CA, USA).

Cell Counting Kit-8 (CCK-8) assay

CCK-8 assay was used to assess the vitality of rat cardiac fibroblasts. The cells were seeded into 96-well plates at a density of 5×103 cells per well and subjected to various treatments. Following a 48-hour incubation, 10 µL of CCK-8 solution was added to each well. Following this, the optical density at 450 nm was measured using a microplate reader to evaluate the viability of cell proliferation.

Colony formation assay

A colony formation assay was performed using rat atrial fibroblasts to evaluate the effects of 1 µM of ang II treatment for 48 hours and transfection with TIMP1 overexpression plasmids and siRNAs. Posttreatment, cells were seeded in a six-well plate at a density of 5×103 cells per well and cultured at 37 ℃ in an atmosphere containing 5% CO2. Subsequently, the fibroblasts were fixed with methanol 15 minutes after the treatment. They were then stained with alkaline nitro blue tetrazolium chloride for 30 minutes, and the formation of evident colonies was observed using a stereomicroscope (Olympus, Tokyo, Japan). Each colony was photographed, and the rate of clonogenic development was quantified.

Statistical analysis

All experiments were performed three times in biological replicates, and the results are expressed as the mean ± standard deviation. Statistical analysis was carried out using R language packages (The R Foundation for Statistical Computing).

The Student t-test was used for pairwise comparisons, while one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA), followed by the Tukey post hoc test, was employed for multiple-group comparisons. Statistical significance was set at a level of P value <0.05.

Results

Enrichment analysis of 224 DEGs in the GSE115574 dataset

The GEO2R tool identified 113 upregulated DEGs and 111 down-regulated DEGs from the GSE115574 dataset (Figure 1A, Table S1). Subsequently, functional enrichment analysis was performed on 224 DEGs. In GO terms (Figure 1B), we found that DEGs were significantly enriched in biological processes associated with ECM, particularly collagen fibril organization and ECM organization. These results highlight the critical role of the ECM in the structural integrity of the heart and its dysregulation in AF. In addition, response to fibroblast growth factor was also enriched, suggesting that growth factors may influence cell proliferation and differentiation and thus be involved in the pathogenesis of AF. Within cellular components (CC) terms (Figure 1C), the DEGs were predominantly associated with the collagen-containing ECM and microfibrils. This suggests the importance of collagen and microfibrillar structures in maintaining cardiac tissue architecture and that their dysregulation may lead to the fibrotic changes observed in AF. Molecular functional (MF) analyses revealed genes enriched in platelet-derived growth factor binding, titin binding, and kinase activity. These molecular functions are critical for cell signaling, cytoskeletal integrity, and enzyme activity, and their dysregulation may affect cellular processes in AF (Figure 1D). Moreover, the KEGG pathway analysis identified significant enrichment in the pathways of myocardial contraction, ECM-receptor interaction, and focal adhesion. These pathways are essential for cardiac function and the maintenance of tissue integrity. Their dysregulation may lead to the electrophysiological and structural remodeling observed in AF, thereby contributing to the pathophysiology of the disease (Figure 1E).

Identification of key intersection genes and expression analysis in AF samples

In this study, we comprehensively analyzed the proteins encoded by 224 DEGs using PPI networks. The MCC, MNC, and degree algorithms identified three key gene modules, each characterized by 10 nodes and 45, 39, and 38 edges, respectively (Figure 2A-2C). As depicted in Figure 2D, we screened 7 key intersecting genes from these modules. We then analyzed the expression of these intersecting genes—BGN, COL1A1, COL1A2, COL3A1, FMOD, THBS2, and TIMP1—in the GSE115574 and GSE14975 datasets. The results, presented in Figure 2E,2F, indicated that these 7 genes are highly expressed in AF samples, suggesting their potential role in the etiology of AF. Among these, TIMP1 was selected as the hub gene for further analysis.

ang II induced the upregulation of rat cardiac fibroblasts’ expression of TIMP1 and activated fibrotic signaling

We used qRT-PCR assay to analyze the expression of TIMP1 in rat cardiac fibroblasts treated with ang II at various doses (1 nM, 10 nM, 100 nM, and 1 µM) and durations (12, 24, and 48 hours). Following 48 hours of treatment with 1 µM of ang II, we observed maximum messenger RNA (mRNA) expression of TIMP1, which increased in a dose- and time-dependent manner (Figure 3A,3B).

Western blotting analysis showed significant upregulation of TGF-β1, α-SMA, p-Smad3, and p-Smad2 in cells treated with 1 µM of ang II for 48 hours (Figure 3C,3D). Notably, the non-phosphorylated forms of Smad2 and Smad3 did not exhibit significant changes upon ang II treatment. These findings suggest a potential mechanistic link between ang II stimulation and the activation of fibrotic signaling pathways in AF, with TIMP1 acting as a major mediator.

Regulation of TIMP1 expression affected the ang II-induced proliferation of rat atrial fibroblasts

We transfected ang II-induced rat atrial fibroblasts with plasmids designed for TIMP1 knockdown and overexpression. TIMP1 knockdown and overexpression efficiency were evaluated using qRT-PCR and Western blotting analysis (Figure 4A-4D). Among the siRNAs tested, si-TIMP1-1 demonstrated the best knockdown efficiency and was chosen for further study. Following 48 hours of treatment with 1 µM of ang II, we assessed TIMP1 expression in atrial fibroblasts. As shown in Figure 4E, qRT-PCR analysis showed that TIMP1 expression increased with the addition of ang II, decreased with knockdown, and increased again after TIMP1 overexpression. In addition, we evaluated the proliferation activity and colony formation of cardiac fibroblasts after different treatments via CCK-8 and colony formation assays. The results in Figure 4F,4G show that ang II treatment induced upregulation of cell viability and increased the colony number as compared with the control condition. Knockdown of TIMP1 resulted in decreased cell viability and colony number, while overexpression of TIMP1 resulted in a significant increase of both indicators (Figure 4F,4G). These findings suggest that TIMP1 regulates the effects of ang II on cardiac fibroblasts proliferation.

TIMP1 regulated the expression of fibrosis markers through the TGF-β/Smad pathway

Using qRT-PCR and Western blotting analyses, we assessed the relative quantities of fibrotic markers—collagen I, α-SMA, and collagen III—in atrial fibroblasts after a 48-hour treatment with 1 µM of ang II. This treatment led to elevated mRNA and protein expression levels of collagen I, α-SMA, and collagen III upon the addition of ang II. Notably, the highest expression levels were observed after TIMP1 overexpression. In contrast, TIMP1 knockdown resulted in decreased expression of these markers (Figure 5A-5C). The Western blotting analysis of TGF-β1, phosphorylated Smad2/3 (p-Smad2/3), and total Smad2/3 further supported these findings (Figure 5D,5E). These results indicate that TIMP1 regulates the expression of fibrosis-related markers through the TGF-β/Smad signaling pathway, thereby influencing the fibrotic processes in cardiac fibroblasts.

Discussion

AF is a common arrhythmia characterized by rapid and irregular contractions of the atria, and its mechanisms and treatments have been the subject of extensive research (22). Atrial fibrosis, a change in the organization of the atria, is an important factor in the development and persistence of AF. Cardiac fibrosis provides a structural substrate for the development of AF by altering the atria’s electrophysiological properties (23). Numerous studies have highlighted a close association between alterations in TIMP and MMP tissue inhibitors and the upregulation of collagen. Yuan et al. observed a downregulation of microRNA-1 (miRNA-1) and an upregulation of microRNA-21 (miRNA-21) in older adults with AF that correlated with changes in myocardial fibrosis, cell apoptosis, and key genes associated with atrial remodeling, such as TIMP1, MMP9, BCL-2, and Bax (24). A study by Yuan et al. showed that Quyu Fange, a Chinese medicine formula, affects rats with hypertensive heart failure caused by diabetes and high blood pressure, which preserves ejection fraction (25). This was attributed to the modification of the ang II/TGF-β1/Smad2/3 signaling pathway, attenuation of atrial and ventricular remodeling, circumvention of myocardial fibrosis and hypertrophy, and marked enhancement of cardiac function.

Based on these findings, we conducted a study in which we thoroughly analyzed the GSE115574 dataset and performed DEG screening and enrichment analysis. It was found that DEGs related to AF were primarily involved in collagen fibrils and the organization of the ECM. This connection underscores the significance of structural remodeling in AF, particularly the production and enrichment of microfibrils and ECM receptor interactions, indicating a complex interplay between cell adhesion and signaling. Furthermore, as the pathways are linked to protein digestion and absorption, as well as myocardial contraction, this suggests that metabolic and contractile dysfunction might contribute to the initiation and persistence of AF. These findings indicate the potential of these pathways to serve as biomarkers or therapeutic targets for the heart remodeling associated with AF. Subsequent analysis identified seven key intersection genes (BGN, COL1A1, COL1A2, COL3A1, FMOD, THBS2, and TIMP1) that were highly expressed in AF samples. This finding was corroborated by analysis of the GSE14975 dataset, underscoring the close association between these genes and AF.

In addition to TIMP1, other genes associated with AF have been identified. Among them, COL1A1, COL1A2, and COL3A1 are all crucial structural proteins within the ECM (26). Dysregulation of these collagen genes and the ensuing fibrosis have been shown to cause abnormal atrial structure, potentially inducing or perpetuating AF. Through bioinformatics analysis, Zhou et al. identified FMOD as being associated with AG comorbidity and significantly associated with ECM and nicotinamide metabolism, among other processes (27). In a study of 18 patients, Van den Berg et al. observed that the elevated expression of COL1A1, COL3A1, THBS2, and BGN may be associated with the sporadic onset of AF (28).

ang II is a peptide hormone essential to the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (29). The transformation of ang I to ang II is carried out by the ang-converting enzyme (30). It acts as a potent vasoconstrictor, leading to an increase in blood pressure by a narrowing of the blood vessels. Moreover, ang II has been implicated in cardiovascular remodeling, inflammation, and fibrosis (31). TGF-β, belonging to the TGF-β superfamily, is a multifunctional cytokine essential for tissue repair, immunological response, and embryonic development (32). The TGF-β/Smad signaling pathway is frequently linked to the development of atrial fibrosis in the setting of AF, which contributes to the formation of arrhythmogenic substrate in the atria (33). Furthermore, Xing et al. demonstrated the potent antifibrotic effects of Decoction of White Aconite in an isoproterenol (ISO)-induced cardiac fibrosis rat model, potentially operating through the Gal-3/TGF-β/Smad signaling pathway by reducing metabolic disturbances and preventing collagen formation (34). The review by Luo et al. indicated that TGF-β promotes atrial fibrosis by activating the SMAD and HESMAD signaling pathways, leading to atrial structural remodeling, thereby increasing the risk of AF (35). We used ang II to induce rat atrial fibroblasts to simulate the pathological state of AF. In vitro experimental results showed that ang II induced upregulation of TIMP1 expression in rat cardiac fibroblasts in a dose- and time-dependent manner. This may be attributed to the activation of the TGF-β/Smad signaling pathway (a key regulator of cardiac fibrosis) by ang II treatment, thereby positively regulating the expression of TIMP1, a downstream effector molecule of the pathway. This time dependence may be related to the accumulation or stabilization of certain components in the signal transduction pathway. In the future, we will continue to explore these potential mechanisms and study the suitability of TIMP1 as a therapeutic target for AF.

In the context of cardiac tissue, collagen I and collagen III, as pivotal ECM proteins, perform vital functions in preserving the heart’s normal structure and functionality (36). Excessive synthesis of these proteins can lead to fibrosis, impacting cardiac structure and function (37). Myocardial fibrosis, a pathological process of structural remodeling in AF, has been extensively studied. A study by Nenna et al. found that collagen I and collagen III were upregulated in patients with postoperative AF, leading to increased myocardial fibrosis, which may be mediated by TGF-β signaling and ultimately increase the risk of postoperative AF (38). Liang et al. detected the up-regulation of TGF-β1, α-SMA, collagen I, and collagen III in an AF model, which was accompanied by aggravated atrial fibrosis. Adding glycyrrhetinic acid (GA) could inhibit the expression of these fibrosis markers, thereby alleviating AF (39). A study by Yao et al. revealed that downregulation of MIAT reduces the atrial fibrosis induced by AF, which is attributed to decreased levels of collagen I, collagen III, CTGF, and TGF-β1 (40). Additionally, Odeh et al. reported that a significant increase in collagen remodeling markers was significantly correlated with inflammatory markers, suggesting that inflammation may promote AF by regulating collagen metabolism. In addition, abnormal collagen remodeling may lead to atrial fibrosis and cardiac structural remodeling, thereby increasing the susceptibility to AF (41). Our experiments assessed the impact of ang II and the effects of both TIMP1 knockdown and overexpression on collagen I, collagen III, and the proteins associated with the TGF-β/Smad pathway. We found that TIMP1 modulates the expression of fibrosis-related markers through the TGF-β/Smad3 pathway. These findings support the use of TIMP1 as a target for AF therapy. By regulating TIMP1 expression or its function in cardiac fibrosis, it may help to slow or reverse the progression of AF. Our findings help to clarify the intricate network of molecular mechanisms involved in cardiac fibrosis regulation.

Targeting TIMP1 in AF treatment faces several challenges. TIMP1 is involved in multiple physiological processes, so inhibiting it could cause unintended side effects. Additionally, the body might compensate by upregulating other related pathways, potentially negating the therapeutic effects. AF’s heterogeneity means TIMP1 may affect different patient subgroups variably, making it crucial to identify who would benefit most from such therapies. Finally, developing delivery systems that specifically target the atrial myocardium without systemic side effects is essential for minimizing off-target interactions. Despite these challenges, several strategies could be employed. Small molecule inhibitors could target TIMP1’s active site to reduce fibrosis without disrupting other functions. Gene therapy using siRNA or CRISPR/Cas9 could selectively knock down TIMP1 expression in cardiac fibroblasts. Monoclonal antibodies could neutralize TIMP1 activity if they are highly specific and effective. Combining TIMP1 inhibitors with other therapies, such as anti-inflammatory drugs, could enhance efficacy. Identifying biomarkers to predict patient response to TIMP1-targeted therapies could also facilitate personalized treatment strategies.

Our study has several limitations. First, it relied on in vitro experiments using rat cardiac fibroblasts, which may not fully reflect the human cardiac environment. Second, proliferation detection was limited to CCK-8 and colony formation assays; direct methods like EdU staining and Ki67 immunofluorescence could provide a more comprehensive assessment. Third, the limited sample size may not represent the broader patient population. Fourth, we focused on short-term effects of ang II on TIMP1 expression, leaving long-term effects unknown. Fifth, lacking direct evidence from human AF patient samples, such as TIMP1 immunohistochemistry or ELISA data, limits clinical correlation. Finally, other molecules and signaling pathways may also be involved in AF pathogenesis. Future work will include in vivo studies, additional proliferation assays, human data collection, and exploring TIMP1’s interactions and regulation.

Conclusions

Our study provides insights into the role of TIMP1 in AF, particularly highlighting its crucial role in the ang II-induced proliferation and fibrosis of atrial fibroblasts. Through a comprehensive analysis of gene expression profiles and functional experiments within the PPI network, TIMP1 was identified as a hub gene significantly associated with AF. In vitro studies found that the regulation of TIMP1 expression affected key pathways, specifically the TGF-β1 and Smad2/3 signaling, thereby influencing the response of atrial fibroblasts to ang II. Modulation of TIMP1 expression significantly affected cell proliferation and the expression of fibrosis markers under ang II stimulation, emphasizing TIMP1’s potential as a therapeutic target for alleviating AF fibrosis and its complications. This study provides a theoretical basis for TIMP1 as a potential biomarker, which may contribute to future improvements in clinical practice and patient management, particularly in assessing treatment efficacy and disease progression. Targeting TIMP1 may enable the development of new therapies that could slow the progression of atrial fibrosis and provide new options for patients unresponsive to existing treatments. Further studies are needed to elucidate the specific mechanism of action of TIMP1 in AF and to evaluate the safety and efficacy of TIMP1-targeted therapeutic strategies in clinical trials.
